Zakat – The Third Pillar of Islam

Introduction

Zakat, the third pillar of Islam, is obligatory for every financially capable Muslim whose income meets or exceeds the prescribed threshold (Nisaab) after essential expenses. It fosters social justice, compassion, and solidarity within the Muslim community.

Meaning of Zakat

The literal meaning of Zakat is ‘purity,’ ‘increment,’ and ‘blessing.’ It purifies wealth and brings blessings to the giver's remaining wealth.

A Monetary Act of Worship

Zakat ensures wealth does not circulate only among the rich, addressing the needs of the less fortunate and promoting societal balance. It is not a favor to the poor but a duty commanded by Allah Almighty.

Significance of Zakat

Zakat is mentioned alongside Salah 32 times in the Quran, highlighting its importance. It offers abundant blessings in this world and the Hereafter and serves as a reminder to share wealth responsibly.

Types of Zakat

  • Zakat of Wealth:
    • Gold and Silver
    • Trade goods, livestock, crops, and fruits (Ushr)
  • Zakat of People: Sadaqa-tul-Fitr

Rulings on Zakat

Zakat involves giving 2.5% of wealth annually to specific recipients. Obligatory for Muslims meeting the Nisaab threshold, it promotes spiritual growth and societal welfare. Variations exist across Islamic schools of thought, requiring consultation with knowledgeable sources.

Virtues & Benefits of Zakat

  • Completes one’s Iman
  • Brings mercy and help from Allah
  • Leads to Taqwa and asceticism
  • Ensures success
  • Purifies wealth
  • Provides blessings in both worlds
  • Safeguards wealth and protects from harm
  • Fosters brotherhood and community welfare

Recipients of Zakat

The Quran outlines the following categories:

  1. The poor (Fuqara)
  2. The destitute (Masakeen)
  3. Zakat collectors
  4. Slaves (to free them)
  5. Debtors
  6. In the cause of Allah
  7. Travellers in need

Note: The category of slaves is no longer applicable in modern times.
